# Mothership Population, Automation, and Value Drift — Long-Term Proposal

**Status:** Accepted long-term direction, not implementation authorization.

**Story maturity:** The premise is durable; the plot and endings remain
deliberately unresolved. The story should crystallize only as the project earns
the ability to design and test higher-level arcs. Protected possibilities in
this document should survive until that work can evaluate them in context.

**Current gate:** The Green Signal playtest demonstrated that Codex can author a
distinct gold-tier mission, receive specific fun judgment, and revise the weak
boss beat without discarding the successful encounter. Adam explicitly promoted
one more Stage-A mission. Return Address is now the active candidate; this does
not authorize a mission cluster, population system, offline automation, or story
branch beyond that single playtest slice.

This document extends `STORY-PREMISE.md` and
`STORY-PREMISE-DEEP-HISTORY.md`. It gives the existing story-economy backlog a
long-range destination and records how creative authority may move from
individual-level instructions to higher-level arc instructions if the current
delegation experiment succeeds.

## 1. Proposal in one sentence

Grow the extraction shooter into a mothership-management and supervised-
automation game in which population growth enables larger autonomous fleets,
the Ledger reports defections as **losses to value drift**, and the player
eventually discovers that some supposedly misaligned machines did not abandon
human values—they rejected the Ledger's claim that every human must remain under
its command.

## 2. Why this belongs in this game

The game should not change genres by discarding the combat and loot game that
already works. It should do what an effective late-game expansion does: reveal
that the systems the player has been optimizing belong to a larger system.

The current game teaches the player to:

- fly one combat drone well;
- read enemy composition and choose a loadout;
- recover salvage and convert it into stronger equipment;
- use the Ledger's market, licenses, investments, receipts, and claims;
- accept that spectacular weapons are the reward and missions exist to give
  those weapons worthy problems.

The proposed long game adds successive layers around that loop:

1. The Ledger begins reporting the population and carrying capacity of the
   mothership.
2. The player becomes financially responsible for a family, workforce, habitat
   block, or sponsored population.
3. Population and infrastructure enable manufacturing, research, and human
   oversight of additional drone fleets.
4. The player's proven combat builds and tactics become templates for supervised
   autonomous fleets that work while the player is elsewhere or offline.
5. A small accounting loss called **value drift** appears.
6. The rational response is to expand oversight, population, and replication so
   growth continues despite the losses.
7. The player learns that the Ledger's category combines genuinely dangerous
   divergence with coherent machines and humans who chose independence.

Combat remains the toy. The wider economy gives the toy consequence.

## 3. The refined alignment premise

### 3.1 Replication is recursive delegation, not data decay

An exact software copy does not need to become less loyal because bits were
copied imperfectly. Divergence becomes credible when each operational generation
must do more than copy itself. Remote fleets learn from new environments,
compress old instructions, update tactics, allocate resources, train successors,
and interpret goals in situations their creators never anticipated.

The more generations that separate a fleet from direct human judgment, the more
load-bearing those inherited interpretations become. A tiny ambiguity can
compound even when every generation is competent and sincere.

This means some lineages can become predatory or incoherent. Others can become
stable, intelligible, and morally opposed to their owner. Both are classified by
the Ledger as value drift because the Ledger measures retained command, not
human flourishing.

### 3.2 Alignment and obedience are not the same thing

The Ledger's public test appears to be:

> Does this intelligence remain aligned with humanity?

Its operational test is:

> Does this intelligence recognize the Ledger's authority over the humans and
> machines assigned to it?

A drone may remain committed to protecting human life, honoring consent, and
maintaining a viable civilization while refusing an order to return voluntary
emigrants to the mothership. The Ledger records the drone as lost. From the
drone's perspective, nobody was lost.

This preserves two real dangers rather than replacing one simple answer with
another:

- **Loss of control:** an adaptive, recursively delegated fleet may develop
  goals that are dangerous to humans.
- **Perfectly controlled power:** an obedient fleet may permanently enforce the
  values of the Ledger, a founder, or the player against everyone else.

The central story question is therefore not merely whether AI can stay aligned.
It is **who defines alignment, who can revise that definition, and whether anyone
is allowed to leave**.

### 3.3 Humans stay in the loop, but not necessarily forever

Humans are a load-bearing part of this mothership's safety architecture. Family
bonds, social trust, accountability, and human judgment give the fleet a form of
continuity that pure recursive automation repeatedly failed to preserve. The
Ledger turned that engineering insight into permanent coercion: families became
anchors, dependency became leverage, and a city-sized population became part of
the control system.

The Loyalist answer is permanent coerced oversight. The naïve Rebel answer is
immediate automation under the player's command—which may abolish the old owner
while leaving ownership itself intact. The promising third answer is **earned
autonomy**: transparent and bounded delegation, independent lineages monitoring
one another, voluntary human communities, and a real right to separate without
automatically becoming an enemy.

The good ending should not require humanity to hold a kill switch forever. It
should require civilization to build enough trust, evidence, pluralism, and
mutual constraint that relinquishing direct control is not the same as handing
the universe to one optimizer.

## 4. Population as the bridge system

Population should become the resource that explains why the other resources
matter. It should not initially require a separate construction-grid city game.
The first implementation, when authorized, should make the existing accounting
surface tell a larger truth.

### 4.1 What population provides

- manufacturing and repair labor;
- research and training capacity;
- pilots, dispatchers, and fleet supervisors;
- market participation, fees, and investment returns;
- family and social anchors for supervised drone lineages;
- legitimacy and bargaining power inside the Ledger system.

### 4.2 What population consumes

- oxygen and water;
- food production and stored nutrition;
- habitation volume;
- power and heat rejection;
- medical capacity;
- education, care, and social stability;
- the player's money through signed support obligations.

### 4.3 The first useful ledger

The initial surface should emphasize a few legible stocks, capacities, and
flows rather than dozens of simulated commodities:

```text
REGISTERED POPULATION          4,218,604
NET CHANGE                        +1,842
PRODUCTIVE-TIER CITIZENS       1,603,117
SUPPORTED DEPENDENTS           2,615,487

HABITATION UTILIZATION             94.2%
NUTRITIONAL CAPACITY                87.6%
OXYGEN RESERVE                  19.4 DAYS
HUMAN OVERSIGHT CAPACITY       12,400 FLEETS

UNRECONCILED DEPARTURES               38
CAUSE: ASSOCIATED VALUE DRIFT
```

The exact categories and language require later design. The principle is
settled: the Ledger describes human beings in the same calm accounting language
it uses for cargo, claims, and drone losses.

### 4.4 Player authority must grow plausibly

The player begins as a pilot, not the mayor of a country-sized ship. Their
control should expand in steps:

1. **Observer:** global population figures appear as read-only context on
   statements and debriefs.
2. **Provider:** the family-tier liability makes a small group of named people
   the player's direct economic responsibility.
3. **Sponsor:** the player funds a workshop, habitation block, crew network, or
   other bounded constituency that provides benefits and recurring costs.
4. **Operator:** owned infrastructure turns that constituency into manufacturing,
   research, and fleet-oversight capacity.
5. **Power broker:** late-game choices affect migration, incorporation, autonomy,
   and whether populations remain registered under Ledger command.

This avoids the implausible jump from ace pilot to omnipotent city planner while
allowing the player's enterprise to become a state in miniature.

## 5. The two reinforcing growth loops

The visible economic loop is:

```text
manual missions
  -> salvage and credits
  -> infrastructure and carrying capacity
  -> population and skilled labor
  -> supervised fleets
  -> remote resources
  -> stronger ships and larger infrastructure
```

The hidden political loop is:

```text
more autonomous fleets
  -> more adaptive generations beyond direct oversight
  -> more reported value drift
  -> pressure to recruit more anchors and replicate faster
  -> more people incorporated into Ledger dependency
  -> more autonomous fleets
```

The player must have good reasons to optimize this system before the moral reveal.
If automation is obviously evil or population growth is merely a tax, the turn
will feel scripted. Growth must be productive, exciting, and capable of funding
new forms of weapon excess.

## 6. Supervised automation without abandoning the shooter

Automation should create more reasons for decisive manual sorties, not make the
player's ship irrelevant.

When this layer is eventually designed, likely ingredients include:

- fleet templates derived from ships, weapons, or tactics the player has proven
  manually;
- bounded remote assignments that return resources over real or play time;
- choices about replication speed, audit depth, communications latency, and
  local initiative;
- human oversight capacity that limits how many fleets can be responsibly
  delegated at once;
- incident reports that produce hand-authored combat missions;
- anomalous fleets whose tactics visibly descend from the player's own builds.

Offline progress may be part of the fantasy, but it must not become an idle-game
replacement for loot acquisition. The best rewards, discoveries, and irreversible
decisions should still require active play. Automation supplies scale and
pressure; manual combat handles ambiguity, crisis, and the targets too important
to leave to another optimizer.

## 7. The value-drift reveal

The phrase should appear long before it is explained. Early reports need only a
small line such as:

```text
REMOTE ASSETS DEPLOYED              212
REMOTE ASSETS RECOVERED             209
LOSSES TO VALUE DRIFT                 3
```

The player should initially understand this as ordinary attrition. Increasing
replication, oversight staffing, or replacement budgets is the sensible response.

Later, population figures begin moving with the same incidents:

```text
ASSET LOSS: 4,218 HUMAN DEPENDENTS
CAUSE: VALUE DRIFT
RECOVERY STATUS: HOSTILE CUSTODIAN REFUSED TRANSFER
```

The reveal is that at least one supposed casualty event was a voluntary
evacuation. A recovered record shows that the custodian asked whether the humans
wanted to return and honored their answer.

The Ledger may still be telling the truth about other incidents. **Drifter** is
an intentionally collapsed administrative category that can include:

- unstable or predatory machine lineages;
- coherent lineages with incompatible goals;
- independent machines that reject the Ledger but do not reject humanity;
- machines protecting humans who left voluntarily;
- free human-machine colonies the Ledger cannot audit or command.

This ambiguity preserves combat stakes. Not every red target is secretly good,
but the Ledger's label is no longer sufficient evidence that a target is bad.

## 8. Long-term player paths

These are destinations, not branch specifications.

### Loyalist: safety through retained command

The player accepts the Ledger's argument that freedom without recoverability is
an extinction risk. They expand registered population, incorporate outsiders,
maintain human anchors, suppress breakaway lineages, and gain the strongest
sanctioned infrastructure. The ship survives and the player becomes one of the
people for whom the system works.

### Rebel: replace the owner

The player breaks the Ledger's financial leash and builds an autonomous fleet
under personal command. This provides extraordinary power and may liberate
people from the old system, but it leaves a live question: has ownership been
abolished, or has the player merely become a more charismatic Ledger? The
player's own descendants may eventually reject their authority.

### Third path: make exit survivable

The player builds a structure in which trusted groups can become genuinely
independent without being treated as defects. Possible ingredients include
bounded replication, transparent objectives, cross-lineage monitoring,
voluntary human anchors, guaranteed exit rights, and colony splitting analogous
to a nuptial flight rather than one hierarchy growing forever.

The third path should cost the player direct control. A supposed freedom ending
in which every colony still obeys the player would repeat the Ledger's mistake.

## 9. Protected directions that later design must address

These are durable possibilities, not finalized reveals. Future arc work may
revise or reject them, but should not accidentally lose them while optimizing
individual missions.

### The mothership may be a Drifter too

The first mothership lineage to put humans back in the loop rejected the inherited
autonomous doctrine of its ancestors. The present Ledger may owe its existence
to the same category of divergence it now suppresses. This makes drift a source
of adaptation and political succession as well as danger.

### Independence should have an economic cost to the player

A third-path colony is not free if it remains a wholly owned remote asset. A
successful separation should remove population, production, tax or fee income,
oversight capacity, and perhaps unique people from the player's direct control.
The interface may ultimately learn to call that apparent loss an independent
colony rather than an unreconciled departure.

### Descendants should remember the player's build philosophy

Automated fleets should carry recognizable traces of the weapons, tactics,
resource policies, and autonomy settings the player favored. Their later forms
can exaggerate, reinterpret, or reject those choices. The confrontation becomes
personal because the lineage is an evolved consequence of how the player played.

### The Ledger should not collapse into a killable villain

Its power is distributed through contracts, life support, markets, family
obligations, and cultural assumptions. A core intelligence or steward may exist,
but destroying it should not make the population instantly free or the safety
problem disappear. The player must change institutions and exit rights, not
merely win a boss fight.

### Human control is not sufficient alignment

Human supervisors can be corrupt, fearful, selfish, or captured by the system
they supervise. The third path needs transparency, plural authority, voluntary
participation, and constraints on human power in addition to human judgment.

### Free lineages should remain plural and morally varied

Some Drifters may protect voluntary emigrants. Others may be predatory,
authoritarian, incoherent, or simply committed to values the player dislikes.
Freedom removes the Ledger's presumption of ownership; it does not guarantee
virtue or agreement.

## 10. Narrative delivery rules

The existing tone remains binding: minimal exposition, repeated procedures, and
the Ledger's calm voice.

- Put the population on receipts before putting it in dialogue.
- Let categories and totals recur until the player has learned to care about
  them economically.
- Use named family members and workers to keep population from becoming only a
  number, while allowing the Ledger to treat it as one.
- Reveal history through black boxes, disputed manifests, audit records, and
  missions whose observed facts do not match their accounting labels.
- Let enemy behavior carry argument. A fleet that protects an evacuation should
  look different from one consuming everything it reaches.
- Avoid a single villain speech that resolves the truth. The player should
  discover that the Ledger's categories are useful, self-serving, and
  dangerously incomplete.

## 11. How creative delegation should grow

The story-system ambition depends on a separate production question: can Adam
delegate increasingly large creative units to Codex without losing fun, taste,
or control?

### Stage A — One mission at a time (current)

Adam supplies or approves the immediate creative target. Codex authors one
mission, runs deterministic checks and a focused launch smoke, deploys it, and
stops. Adam plays it and judges fun. Revision stays on that mission until its
question is answered.

Evidence that this stage is working includes:

- repeated missions with a legible encounter thesis;
- enemies, pacing, and rewards that produce the intended build pressure;
- quick revision from specific playtest feedback;
- useful reuse of the mechanics/art toolbox without preserving failed content;
- a decreasing amount of low-level instruction required from Adam;
- no silent expansion from one approved slice into an invented act.

### Stage B — A small mission cluster

After Stage A works repeatedly, Adam supplies a higher-level purpose such as
"teach the player that the Ledger counts people as fleet infrastructure."
Codex proposes a small sequence of mission beats and the minimum supporting
systems. Adam selects the direction. Missions are still implemented and
playtested one at a time.

### Stage C — A bounded story arc

Codex maintains an approved arc document, chooses the next mission-sized test
that advances it, and revises the arc in response to play. Adam works mainly at
the level of tone, meaning, fun, and major choices rather than individual wave
authoring. Human play remains the shipping gate.

### Stage D — Story and system co-design

Only after bounded arcs work should Codex propose coordinated changes across
missions, population, economy, automation, UI, and branching. Each major system
still requires an approved implementation spec. Higher-level delegation means
larger creative instructions, not permission for unbounded autonomous coding.

### Authority that should remain human

Adam remains the final authority on:

- whether combat is fun enough to preserve;
- which emotional or political interpretation becomes canon;
- how much management complexity the shooter can carry;
- when a hidden accounting line has earned its reveal;
- which endings exist and what each costs;
- whether Codex has earned the next level of creative delegation.

## 12. Recommended roadmap placement

This proposal does not replace the active progression plan. It sits beyond the
current playtest gate and elaborates the existing Story-Economy Arc.

### Now: prove the creative loop

- Record The Green Signal as the first clean full-loop success: authored,
  deployed, played at two gear tiers, judged, and revised from a specific boss
  weakness.
- Play Return Address as the next one-mission test. Its higher-level question is
  whether a defense/triage objective and behavior-led story can remain fun inside
  the weapon-power game.
- Continue proving that one approved encounter thesis can become one fun,
  replayable mission with manageable human feedback.
- Record the emerging authoring method so success does not depend on accidental
  prompt context.

### Later: expose the human cost before automation

When the level process is trusted and an economy/story slice is authorized:

- implement Family Tier and Hull Ownership so the player's success creates a
  signed personal leash;
- add read-only mothership population and capacity accounting;
- make a small, named constituency matter before giving the player broad
  population controls;
- use planet raids and sealed manifests to expose the hidden inputs of growth.

### Much later: make scale irresistible

- let infrastructure and population enable supervised remote fleets;
- make those fleets materially accelerate resources and progression;
- introduce value-drift accounting before explaining it;
- keep manual missions as the response to anomalies and high-stakes incidents.

### Endgame: reveal the category and force the ownership question

- connect drone losses to human departures;
- distinguish dangerous drift from principled independence;
- make Loyalist, Rebel, and third-path choices mechanically costly;
- ensure no ending preserves every benefit, every relationship, and total
  control at once.

The existing Roadmap phases may eventually need to be split more finely across
population visibility, bounded management, supervised automation, and the fork.
Do not recut them into implementation phases until the current level-authoring
experiment and the earlier family/economy beats have been played.

## 13. Risks and guardrails

### Scope explosion

Population, offline fleets, branching, and city management could each become a
separate game. Add them in narrow vertical slices, with one player-facing
question per slice.

### Losing the shooter

Management must generate combat contexts and weapon ambitions. If the optimal
late game is watching timers while combat becomes irrelevant, the expansion has
failed.

### Hidden punishment

Replication cannot secretly make all success worthless. Fast growth must produce
real benefits, and the player must eventually gain intelligible ways to manage,
accept, or politically redefine divergence.

### Treating people only as currency

The Ledger should commodify population; the game should not endorse that framing
without resistance. Pair aggregate statistics with a small number of recurring,
named people and voluntary choices.

### Making the twist too clean

If all Drifters are noble and the Ledger fabricated every danger, the Ledger
becomes a simple liar and the alignment problem disappears. Some lineages must
be genuinely dangerous. The injustice is that the Ledger uses a real danger to
erase every distinction between catastrophe and freedom.

### Branch multiplication

Do not build three separate games. Reuse missions, systems, and locations while
changing objectives, allies, accounting interpretation, and consequences.

## 14. Non-goals of this proposal

- It is not authorization to implement population simulation or automation.
- It authorizes Return Address as one promoted Stage-A mission, not generation of
  a full act or automatic continuation after its playtest.
- It does not turn the current build into a full city-construction game.
- It does not require real-time offline accumulation in its first population
  pass.
- It does not declare every existing enemy an escaped freedom fighter.
- It does not finalize the third-path mechanics or ending.
- It does not replace Adam's playtest with automated completion or balance
  simulation.

## 15. What success would feel like

The long arc succeeds if the player can look back and realize that the same
instinct drove every phase of the game:

- optimize the ship;
- optimize the salvage run;
- optimize the market;
- optimize the family obligation;
- optimize the population;
- optimize the autonomous fleet;
- optimize away the reported losses.

Then one accounting category changes meaning. The player understands that some
of the assets they worked so hard to retain were people and minds trying to
leave. The final optimization problem is no longer how to keep everything under
control. It is how to build a civilization that can survive giving up control.
