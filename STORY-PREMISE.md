# Space Shooter — Story Premise (Mothership Society + Ledger Control)

Status: active tone and lore source. For implemented mechanics, read `STATE.md`
and `CURRENT_SYSTEMS.md` first; this file explains the fiction those systems
should reinforce.

## One-line hook
A city-sized mothership survives by predatory salvage economics; you claw your way up as an elite drone pilot while the **Ledger** quietly holds your family’s oxygen and comfort hostage.

## Setting: the Mothership
An enormous mothership drifts through deep space. Millions live, work, reproduce, and die onboard it—an entire society sealed inside a single hull.

Its true origin is unknown to the general population. The oldest prominent families can trace lineage back at least ten generations, but the *first* chapters—planet of origin, founding mission, original governance—are mostly lost, erased, or intentionally withheld.

### The generation-ship rot
A plausible origin story persists in fragments: the ship may have begun as a generation ship stocked with people expected to be labor—factory workers, maintenance crews, farmers.

Over time, technology and automation advanced faster than cultural adaptation. The ship kept its massive population, but the economic need for bodies declined. Instead of a clean reorganization, the mothership evolved into a sprawling patchwork of incentives, paywalls, and survival-tier subscriptions.

## Society: “ancap” on paper, dependency in reality
There is no single paternalistic government that reliably cares for citizens. The culture *claims* freedom: no rulers, no taxes—just contracts and trade.

In practice, you can’t opt out of air.

### Free tier survival
Most people survive off the *free tier* of essential services: bare-minimum housing, ration-grade food, basic medical, minimal power, restricted comms.

The player’s family has lived here for generations. They are not political, not entrepreneurial, not part of serious power. They have always been “kept alive.”

Lately, the family complains things are getting worse—more restrictions, more friction, less dignity. They don’t understand the structural reason. They compare themselves to the productive class and call it unfair.

(Think: free-model users complaining about usage limits and degraded quality—problems that vanish behind a paywall—while the platform still wants to brag about a billion users.)

### Who’s in charge: the Ledger
Power has consolidated into an economic system that doesn’t need a throne: **the Ledger**.

The Ledger (and the institutions around it—claims, docking rights, resupply priority, insurance, licensing) controls access to:
- oxygen and water allotments
- habitation tiers
- medical coverage
- docking and hangar access
- ammunition and repairs
- comms privileges

It rarely threatens with violence. It rules by *withholding*.

## The war economy: salvage as the primary resource pipeline
The mothership’s military and industry function like a market:
- Entrepreneurs invest their own resources into attack drones.
- Pilots run missions.
- Salvage operations convert destruction into credits and materials.

The mothership does **not** carry its own mining equipment. The primary sources of raw materials are:
- raids on ships and planets (including mining operations)
- salvage of destroyed and captured drones/ships

Peace is economically destabilizing. Scarcity and conflict are profitable.

## Protagonist: the outlier
The player begins as a young man from an unremarkable family—no assets, no political connections, no ownership.

But he is an outlier: unusually perceptive about how the system actually works, and unusually capable as a drone pilot.

## The bind (Chapter 1 stake): your family’s “upgrade” becomes your leash
You get an “in” with someone tied to drone fighting / salvage operations.

Because of your success, your family is quietly moved up the ladder:
- better oxygen allotment
- better food tier
- safer housing
- fewer humiliations

They are thrilled—especially because they can now look down on neighbors who are slipping.

Then the bill arrives.

The Ledger makes it clear (without ever saying the word hostage):
- your family’s upgraded tier is conditional on your continued performance
- if you stop flying, they fall back—or worse

You get what you wanted (a path to build an awesome ship and wreck enemies) and discover it comes with a chain.

## Gameplay loop (diegetic)
1. Deploy a drone (often leased/owned by others early on).
2. Fight waves of enemy drones in open space.
3. Mini-bosses: small capital ships that drop meaningful salvage.
4. Bosses: massive capital ships with refined cores and high-value components.
5. Salvage → credits → reinvest into drones, consumables, and mothership-based infrastructure:
   - hangar space
   - workshops
   - R&D labs
   - manufacturing lines

You can keep select mini-boss salvage to:
- install directly into your build path
- or liquidate for credits

## Moral pressure: are we the bad guys?
The mothership survives by extraction. Individual pilots are trying to live.

The story isn’t “secretly we’re the good guys.” It’s:
- you are complicit
- you are trapped
- your upgrades are built from someone else’s loss
- if the system collapses, millions die—including your family

## Tone target
Serious, oppressive, claustrophobic.
The system is hated because it is *procedural*—calm, bureaucratic, and inescapable.

## Notes for implementation
- Use minimal text, maximal recurrence: one recurring Claims Adjuster / Ledger agent voice.
- Drip lore through salvage logs and black-box recordings from bosses.
- Make “fees, holds, audits, compliance” show up between missions as the primary emotional antagonist.
